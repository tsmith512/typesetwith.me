Leading

A term originating from hand-typesetting, leading (pronounced (“ledd-ing”) refers to the width of thin strips of lead placed between lines of type to increase the spacing between them. In modern digital typography, the term usually refers to the distance from one baseline to the next (and therefore includes both the height of the text plus the spacing between lines) and is frequently called the “line height” in consumer-oriented word processors.

Text set “solid” (no leading) appears cramped, making it difficult for the eye to track from the end of one line to the beginning of the next, and making vertical rivers between words more obvious than the spacing between lines. However, when leading is too wide, lines can also be difficult to track visually, and vertical space is wasted.

(Adapted from Wikipedia)

For body copy, a decent starting place for leading is ¼ to ½ the height of the type, for a total line height of 1.25 to 1.5. This must be adjusted based on the typeface, type size, measure, contrast (difference in color) between the type and the background, and the intended audience.

Please note: the leading of samples in this application does include the total line height. The color-coding of the leading is based on the ratio of the interline space and the type size.
