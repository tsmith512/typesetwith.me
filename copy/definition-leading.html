Leading

A term originating from hand-typesetting, leading (pronounced “LEDD-ing”) refers to the width of thin strips of lead placed between lines of type increase the spacing between them. In modern digital typography, the term more frequently refers to the distance from the baseline of one line of text to the baseline of the next (and therefore includes both the height of the line plus the height of the lead) and is frequently called the “line height” in consumer-oriented word processors.

Text set “solid” (no leading) appears cramped, makes it difficult for the eye to track from the end of one line to the beginning of the next, and makes vertical rivers between words more obvious than the spacing between lines. However, when leading is too high, lines can also be difficult to track visually, and vertical space is wasted.

(Adapted from Wikipedia)

For body copy paragraphs, half of the height of the type is a decent starting place to determine good leading (for a total height 1.5 times the size of the type). This must be adjusted based on the typeface, type size, measure, contrast between the type and the background, and the intended audience.

Please note: the leading of samples in this application does include the total line height. The color-coding of the leading is based on the ratio of the true leading (just the interline space) and the type size.
